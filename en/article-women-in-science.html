Women in science

When literature restores forgotten women

For a long time, women in science were relegated to the background. Today, literature does them justice: through words, it repairs what history erased.

In this article, we see how biographies, novels, and graphic novels become tools of memory: they make forgotten journeys visible and place discoveries back into the collective story.

Literature in service of memory

Science advances through proof.
Literature advances through trace.

And it is often these traces that bring back to life those whom collective memory forgot.

Stories that repair the invisible

Biographies, novels, graphic novels: contemporary works tell the lives of researchers who stayed in the shadows, and put their discoveries back into the larger story of knowledge.

This is another way to write the history of science: more human, fairer. It is not only about results, but about lives, contexts, struggles.

Understanding the Matilda effect

The Matilda effect names a phenomenon: the work of some women scientists was minimized, credited to others, or simply forgotten.

When names disappear

It is not only an individual injustice: it is a cultural mechanism that shapes collective memory, textbooks, even the way we tell history.

Restoring through writing

Writing about these women means resisting oblivion. Words and sentences become evidence: where institutions erased, literature remembers.

  • Biographies: reconstruct a trajectory.
  • Novels: make an era tangible.
  • Comics: transmit differently, more widely.

Stories about Lise Meitner or Rosalind Franklin remind us that behind every discovery there is a person, an intuition, sometimes a struggle.

A bridge between science and culture

Science discovers, literature tells. By giving a place back to those erased, the works build another form of truth: that of memory.

And if the truth always ends up being known, it is perhaps because it always ends up being told.
