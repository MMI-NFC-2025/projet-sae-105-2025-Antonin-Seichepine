Women in Science

When literature restores forgotten women

For a long time, women in science were pushed into the background. Today, literature restores justice to them: through words, it repairs what history has erased.

In this article, we explore how biographies, novels, and graphic novels become tools of memory: they make forgotten journeys visible and place discoveries back into the collective narrative.

Literature in the service of memory

Science advances through evidence.
Literature advances through traces.

And it is often these traces that bring back to life those whom collective memory has forgotten.

Stories that repair the invisible

Biographies, novels, graphic novels: contemporary works tell the lives of women researchers who remained in the shadows, and place their discoveries back into the great narrative of knowledge.

It is another way of writing the history of science: more human, fairer. We do not only talk about results, but about lives, contexts, and struggles.

Understanding the Matilda Effect

The Matilda Effect refers to a phenomenon: the work of some women scientists was minimized, attributed to others, or simply forgotten.

When names disappear

This is not only a matter of individual injustice: it is a cultural mechanism that shapes collective memory, textbooks, and even the way history is told.

Restoring through writing

Writing about these women means resisting oblivion. Words and sentences become evidence: where institutions erased, literature remembers.

  • Biographies: reconstructing a trajectory.
  • Novels: making an era tangible.
  • Graphic novels: transmitting differently, more broadly.

Stories devoted to Lise Meitner or Rosalind Franklin remind us that behind every discovery there is a person, an intuition, sometimes a struggle.

A bridge between science and culture

Science discovers, literature tells. By restoring a place to those who were erased, works build another form of truth: that of memory.

And if truth always ends up being known, perhaps it is because it always ends up being told.
