Nuclear fission

How an atom releases energy

Nuclear fission, clarified in the late 1930s (notably thanks to Lise Meitner), is built on a simple idea: when an atomic nucleus splits, it releases an enormous amount of energy.

In this article, we break down what an atom is, how a chain reaction happens, and how this energy can generate electricity… or weapons.

What is an atom, exactly?

Picture a tiny solar system: a compact nucleus in the center (protons + neutrons), with electrons orbiting around it.

The key point: nuclear stability

Most nuclei are stable. But some, like uranium-235, are more fragile: a single neutron can be enough to destabilize them.

  • Protons: positive charge, in the nucleus.
  • Neutrons: no charge, in the nucleus.
  • Electrons: negative charge, around the nucleus.

When uranium-235 captures a neutron, it becomes unstable and may split. This split releases energy and more neutrons.

Glossary: key concepts

A chain reaction

When a nucleus splits, it releases neutrons. Those neutrons hit other nuclei… which split in turn.

The simplest image: dominos

Think of a row of dominos: once the first falls, everything follows. In fission, each “fall” releases a huge amount of energy.

From atoms to electricity

In a nuclear power plant, fission energy becomes heat. Heat boils water, steam spins turbines, and electricity is produced.

  1. Fission → heat.
  2. Water → pressurized steam.
  3. Turbines → rotation.
  4. Generator → electricity.

Progress and responsibility

The same reaction that can power cities also enabled the atomic bomb. Meitner, who helped explain it, refused to take part.

“The danger is not the atomic bomb, but the heart of man.”

Albert Einstein

From science to cultural narratives

Fission became a symbol of power, fear, and modernity. It still fuels books and films—between fascination and anxiety.

Example: the documentary Atomic Power questions how science can become propaganda, between promise and threat.
