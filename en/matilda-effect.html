The Matilda effect

We talk about the place of women in science by asking a simple question: why do their names disappear?

Explore the timeline

Why do they disappear?

The Matilda effect is a phenomenon in which women scientists are ignored or see their contributions erased from history.

It affects women in STEM, in literature, in sport, and in many other fields.

Let us give them back the place they deserve.

Women in History — Timeline

1647

The French Academy of Sciences refuses to admit women.

1774

Émilie du Châtelet dies, author of a translation of Newton's Principia.

1816

Sophie Germain publishes work on the vibration of metal plates.

1890

Sociologist Harry C. Garrison spreads sexist stereotypes.

1892

A school board cites the supposed "masculinity" of science jobs.

1904

Max Planck argues that women must choose between science and family.

1913

The American Physics Society opposes women's participation.

1914

The British Medical Association refuses Marie Stopes.

1936

U.S. social workers note gender bias in science.

1938

Freeman dismisses women's abilities in science.

1958

Mathematicians in the U.S. criticize women in science.

1962

Male students block women's access to higher education.

1964

Biologist Peter Medawar compares women to "intelligent goldfish".

1967

Compulsory high school science courses exclude girls.

1968

Cambridge scientists say women do not belong in the lab.

1970

Biologist Sewall Wright rejects women's presence in science.

1977

Some academics deem women's work unpublishable.

1979

The Matilda effect is identified: women's contributions are minimized.

1980

A physics professor questions women's ability to understand complex theories.

1981

Marie Stopes fights to stay in the Royal Society archives.

1992

93% of Berkeley students say women's exclusion is long-lasting.

1998

Council of Europe mission recommends targeting sexism in education.
