Nuclear fission

Understanding how an atom releases energy

Nuclear fission, brought to light at the end of the 1930s (notably thanks to Lise Meitner), rests on a simple idea: when an atomic nucleus breaks apart, it releases a huge amount of energy.

In this article, we clarify what an atom is, how a chain reaction begins, then how this energy can generate electricity… or weapons.

What exactly is an atom?

Imagine a tiny solar system: a nucleus at the center (protons + neutrons), and electrons orbiting around it.

What matters here: the stability of the nucleus

Most nuclei are stable. But some, like uranium-235, are more fragile: a single neutron can be enough to disturb them.

  • Protons: positive charge, in the nucleus.
  • Neutrons: no charge, in the nucleus.
  • Electrons: negative charge, around the nucleus.

When uranium-235 captures a neutron, it becomes unstable and can split. This split releases both energy and additional neutrons.

A chain reaction

When a nucleus breaks apart, it releases neutrons. These neutrons strike other nuclei… which break in turn.

The simplest image: dominoes

It is like a row of dominoes: once the first falls, everything follows. In fission, each “fall” releases enormous energy.

From atom to electricity

In a nuclear power plant, the energy from fission becomes heat. This heat boils water: the steam drives turbines, which produce electricity.

  1. Fission → heat generation.
  2. Water → steam under pressure.
  3. Turbines → rotation.
  4. Alternator → electricity.

Fission, between progress and responsibility

This reaction can power entire cities… but it also enabled the atomic bomb. Meitner, who provided its explanation, refused to take part.

"The danger is not the atomic bomb, but the heart of man."

Albert Einstein

From science to collective story

Fission has become a symbol: power, fear, modernity. It still feeds popular culture, especially in cinema.

Example: the documentary Atomic Power questions how science can become a tool of propaganda, between promise and threat.
