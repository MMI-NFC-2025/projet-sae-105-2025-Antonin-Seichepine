Understanding how an atom releases energy

Nuclear fission, brought to light in the late 1930s (notably thanks to Lise Meitner), is based on a simple idea: when an atomic nucleus splits, it releases an immense amount of energy.

In this article, we clarify what an atom is, how a chain reaction begins, and how this energy can produce electricity… or weapons.

What exactly is an atom?

Imagine a tiny solar system. At the center, a compact nucleus, made of protons and neutrons. Around it, electrons orbit like planets around a sun.

Stability and fission

This nucleus is generally stable, but some elements such as uranium 235 are less so. A single neutron can be enough to destabilize it.

When this happens, the nucleus splits into two smaller parts, releasing an immense amount of thermal and light energy: this is the process of nuclear fission.

A chain reaction

When a uranium nucleus splits, it releases not only energy, but also new neutrons. These neutrons strike other nuclei, which then split in turn.

The domino effect

This cycle repeats itself endlessly: this is known as a chain reaction. It can be compared to a row of dominoes: once the first falls, everything follows.

But here, each “fall” releases a gigantic amount of energy on an atomic scale. This principle makes fission both powerful and difficult to control.

From atom to electricity

In a nuclear power plant, the energy released by fission is transformed into heat. This heat boils water, whose steam turns turbines to generate electricity.

Fission, once a laboratory phenomenon, has become one of the pillars of our modern world.

Fission, between progress and responsibility

This reaction, capable of lighting entire cities, also led to the creation of the atomic bomb.

Lise Meitner, who provided its theoretical explanation, refused to take part, convinced that science should enlighten, not destroy.

This moral dilemma still runs through our time: how can knowledge and responsibility be reconciled?

“Scientists have a responsibility for what they discover.”

Albert Einstein

From science to collective narrative

Nuclear fission has not only transformed physics: it has also shaped our collective imagination. A symbol of power and fear, it continues to inspire literature and cinema, between fascination and anxiety.

This is the case with the documentary Atomic Power, which questions how science sometimes becomes a narrative tool, caught between promise and threat.
