Women in science

How literature restores forgotten women

For a long time, women in science were pushed into the background. Today, literature helps restore what history erased: through words, it gives visibility back.

This article explores how biographies, novels, and graphic novels can act as tools of collective memory, bringing forgotten careers back into the shared narrative.

Literature as a tool of memory

Science moves forward through evidence.
Literature moves forward through traces.

And those traces often bring back the women that collective memory left behind.

Stories that make the invisible visible

Biographies, novels, graphic novels: contemporary works tell the lives of researchers who stayed in the shadows, and put their discoveries back into the larger story of knowledge.

It is another way to write the history of science: more human, more fair. Not only results, but lives, contexts, and struggles.

Glossary: key terms

Understanding the Matilda effect

The Matilda effect describes a pattern: women’s scientific work was sometimes minimized, credited to others, or simply forgotten.

When names disappear

This is not only individual injustice: it is a cultural mechanism that shapes textbooks and the way we narrate history.

“Recognition isn’t a reward: it’s a condition for existing in history.”

Restoring through writing

Writing about these women is a way to resist forgetting. Words become evidence: where institutions erased, literature remembers.

  • Biographies: rebuild a trajectory.
  • Novels: make an era tangible.
  • Graphic novels: transmit differently, to wider audiences.

Stories about Lise Meitner or Rosalind Franklin remind us that behind every discovery there is a person, an intuition, and sometimes a fight.

A bridge between science and culture

Science discovers, literature tells. By restoring space to those erased, works build another kind of truth: memory.

And if truth eventually becomes known, it may be because it eventually gets told.
