Lise Meitner

The woman behind nuclear fission

An Austrian physicist of Jewish origin, Lise Meitner (1878–1968) played a key role in the discovery of nuclear fission. Long overlooked, she embodies what is now known as the Matilda effect.

A scientist in a world that didn’t want her

Born in Vienna, Lise Meitner earned in 1906 one of the first physics doctorates awarded to a woman in Austria. The following year, she joined chemist Otto Hahn in Berlin, starting a collaboration that lasted over thirty years at the Kaiser Wilhelm Institute.

But in 1938, the rise of Nazism forced her to flee Germany. Exiled in Sweden, she continued her work with limited resources.

Fission: a discovery without recognition

Months after her departure, Hahn observed an unexpected phenomenon: the uranium nucleus appeared to split. From Sweden, Meitner explained it: the nucleus divides into two, releasing a tremendous amount of energy. With her nephew Otto Frisch, she published the interpretation in January 1939 and introduced the term nuclear fission.

The discovery transformed physics, yet in 1944 the Nobel Prize in Chemistry was awarded to Hahn alone. Meitner, crucial to understanding the phenomenon, was sidelined.

“Scientists have a responsibility for what they discover.”

Lise Meitner

A rare moral stance

Lise Meitner refused to take part in the Manhattan Project, aware of the risks of scientific progress. She defended an ethical, humanist view of science: research is never neutral—it carries responsibility.

Recognition came late

Only toward the end of her life did the scientific community begin to acknowledge her. In 1966 she received the Enrico Fermi Award alongside Hahn and Strassmann. In 1997, element 109 was named meitnerium (Mt) in her honor.

A memory worth preserving

Lise Meitner’s story questions women’s place in science and the moral responsibility of progress. It reminds us that discovery is not only a scientific fact—it is also a human story, sometimes marked by injustice.
