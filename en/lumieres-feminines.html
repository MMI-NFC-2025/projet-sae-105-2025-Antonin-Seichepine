Giving voice to the forgotten

Feminine Lights

Published in 2024, Feminine Lights gives voice back to those history too often erased. Somewhere between biography and literary reflection, it highlights the journeys of Marie Curie and Lise Meitner, and restores their place in scientific memory.

A story that repairs forgetting

Rather than fixed portraits, the book reveals both the strength and fragility of these women.

Behind every discovery lies a battle: against prejudice, against institutions, against erasure.

Writing becomes a tool of repair — a way to bring light back where textbooks too often erased.

“This book isn’t only about science. It’s about light — the light taken from women when their names are forgotten, and the light rekindled every time we read them.”

Excerpt — Feminine Lights: Marie Curie and Women in Science, 2024

Through these pages, biography becomes an act of memory — and literature becomes justice.

When literature becomes memory

In Feminine Lights, literature works like a laboratory of remembrance. Words and stories become recognition.

Each chapter gives these researchers a voice, a trace, a place in history. The portrait of Lise Meitner, a pioneer of nuclear fission, is a striking example.

Her journey shows how science and literature can speak together to restore a place to those history pushed aside.

A legacy to pass on

By celebrating Marie Curie — a universal symbol of perseverance and curiosity — Feminine Lights does more than honor a figure: it restores a collective legacy.

The book reminds us that knowledge isn’t measured only in discoveries, but also in written traces and shared memory.

To write is to resist forgetting. It is to rekindle the light of those whose flame was hidden.
