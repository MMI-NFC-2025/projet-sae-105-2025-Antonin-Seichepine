Lise Meitner

The woman behind nuclear fission

An Austrian physicist of Jewish origin, Lise Meitner (1878–1968) played a key role in the discovery of nuclear fission. Long ignored, she now embodies what is known as the Matilda Effect.

A scientist in a world that did not want her

Born in Vienna, Lise Meitner obtained in 1906 one of the first doctorates in physics awarded to a woman in Austria. The following year, she joined chemist Otto Hahn in Berlin, beginning a collaboration of more than thirty years at the Kaiser Wilhelm Institute.

But in 1938, the rise of Nazism forced her to flee Germany. Exiled to Sweden, she continued her research under precarious conditions.

Fission: a discovery without recognition

A few months after her departure, Hahn observed an unexpected phenomenon: the uranium nucleus appeared to split. From Sweden, Meitner provided the explanation: the nucleus divides into two, releasing a considerable amount of energy. With her nephew Otto Frisch, she published this interpretation in January 1939 and introduced the term nuclear fission.

This discovery transformed physics. Yet in 1944, the Nobel Prize in Chemistry was awarded to Otto Hahn alone. Lise Meitner, essential to understanding the phenomenon, was left aside.

“Scientists have a responsibility for what they discover.”

Lise Meitner

A rare moral stance

Lise Meitner refused to take part in the Manhattan Project, aware of the possible consequences of scientific progress. She defended an ethical and humanist vision of science: research is never neutral; it entails responsibility.

Late recognition

It was only toward the end of her life that the scientific community began to do her justice. In 1966, she received the Enrico Fermi Award alongside Hahn and Strassmann. In 1997, element 109 was named meitnerium (Mt) in her honor.

A memory to preserve

Lise Meitner’s journey questions the place of women in science and the moral responsibility of progress. It reminds us that every discovery is not only a scientific fact, but also a human story, sometimes marked by injustice.
