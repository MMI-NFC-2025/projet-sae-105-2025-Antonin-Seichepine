Atomic Power

When science becomes propaganda

Released in 1946, the documentary Atomic Power set out to celebrate the victory of progress. But behind its triumphant tone lies another reality: that of science used as a political tool.

This film shows how science can be staged, shaped, and instrumentalized to serve a narrative.

A film that glorifies the bomb

The film shows factories, laboratories, and spectacular explosions. Nuclear fission becomes a symbol of human intelligence and American power.

Everything is designed to make the bomb appear as the natural culmination of progress.

Science at the service of politics

Nothing in Atomic Power is left to chance. Solemn voice-over, heroic music, sharp editing: everything is built to persuade rather than question.

Science becomes a tool of political narrative. It does not explain — it reassures.

This glorification of nuclear power hides dangers, ethical questions, and human consequences.

Nuclear power in cinema

Cinema quickly seized on nuclear power: a symbol of destruction, but also of fascination.

"Science has given us the power to transform the world, but not always the wisdom to guide it."

Carl Sagan

An ambiguous legacy

Atomic Power is not only a propaganda film: it reflects a generation convinced it could dominate nature.

It shows that nuclear power is both a tool of progress and a permanent threat.

A crucial reminder: scientific progress can become dangerous when it lacks moral reflection.
