Atomic Power

When science becomes propaganda

Released in 1946, the documentary Atomic Power aimed to celebrate the victory of progress. But behind its triumphant tone lies another reality: that of science used as a political tool.

This film is a striking example of how science can be staged, shaped, and instrumentalized to serve a narrative.

A film that glorifies the bomb

The film shows factories, laboratories, and spectacular explosions. Nuclear fission becomes a symbol of human intelligence and American power.

Everything is designed to make the bomb appear as the natural outcome of progress.

Science in the service of politics

Nothing in Atomic Power is left to chance. Solemn voice-over, heroic music, fast-paced editing: everything is meant to persuade the audience.

Science becomes a tool of political storytelling. It does not explain — it reassures.

This glorification of nuclear power hides the dangers, the ethical questions, and the human consequences.

Nuclear power in cinema

Cinema quickly embraced nuclear power: a symbol of destruction, but also of fascination.

An ambiguous legacy

Atomic Power is not just a propaganda film: it reflects a generation convinced it could dominate nature.

It shows that nuclear power is both a tool for progress and a permanent threat.

A crucial reminder: scientific progress can become dangerous when it lacks moral reflection.
